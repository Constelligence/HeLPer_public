When conducting a **visual inspection of a tower crane** in a construction planning context, common accessories you should pay attention to include:

2. **Wire ropes** – Inspect for fraying, kinking, corrosion, or improper reeving, since damaged ropes can lead to load failure.  

3. **Load indicators / load moment indicators (LMI)** – Ensure they are functional and calibrated; faulty devices can lead to overloading accidents.  

4. **Boom/jib attachments** – Look for signs of distortion, loose bolts, or cracks in jib sections and connection pins.  

5. **Lighting and warning beacons** – These are crucial for site safety and visibility, particularly if the crane overhangs public roads or workspaces.  

6. **Anti-collision and proximity sensors** – Make sure they are intact and operational, important if the crane is working near other cranes or buildings.  

7. **Cab controls and operator seat harness** – Verify controls are responsive, with no sticking levers or worn harness fittings.  

8. **Limit switches (hoist, trolley, slew)** – Functional limit switches prevent the crane from exceeding safe movement ranges.  

9. **Signage and load charts** – Load capacity plates and operational instructions should be present, legible, and weather-resistant.  

10. **Counterweights and securing bolts** – Ensure counterweights are properly fixed and free from cracks or corrosion, as improper counterweight placement can cause instability.  

From a **local traffic impact perspective**, some of these accessories (like lighting, warning beacons, and signage) are vital when the crane’s jib or load path extends over public roads. Properly functioning safety devices help alert drivers and pedestrians, reducing the risk of accidents. These tests are typically part of pre-installation checks, periodic inspections, and post-assembly verifications.  

Common **non-destructive tests performed by qualified personnel** include:

1. **Visual Testing (VT)**  
   - The first step in NDT, involving a detailed visual inspection of crane components—booms, jibs, welds, pins, and bolts—for deformation, corrosion, or cracking.  
   - Qualified inspectors (often Level II or III NDT technicians) use magnification, lighting, and sometimes drones for high or difficult areas.  

2. **Magnetic Particle Testing (MT)**  
   - Used on ferromagnetic materials to detect surface and near-surface cracks, such as in crane mast sections or weld joints.  
   - The technique uses magnetic fields and iron particles that accumulate at discontinuities, highlighting potential defects.

3. **Ultrasonic Testing (UT)**  
   - Employs high-frequency sound waves to detect internal flaws or discontinuities within structural steel, welds, and load-bearing joints of the crane tower sections or base anchors.  
   - Useful for identifying hidden subsurface defects that could lead to failure under stress.

4. **Dye Penetrant Testing (PT)**  
   - Used on non-ferrous metals (like aluminum components) or painted surfaces after cleaning, to locate surface cracks or porosity in welds and coupler connections.

5. **Radiographic Testing (RT)**  
   - Involves X-rays or gamma rays to detect internal flaws in welds or structural joints.  
   - Generally used for critical connections where maximum reliability is required.

6. **Eddy Current Testing (ET)**  
   - Used to inspect conductive materials—ideal for identifying surface cracks or corrosion under coatings—applied to components such as bonding pins and electrical grounding systems.

### Response 2

Mobile cranes must be equipped with a wide range of safety features to ensure safe operation—both for site workers and surrounding areas such as adjacent roads or pedestrian zones affected by crane movement. These features are designed to prevent equipment failure, overloading, overturning, and accidents during lifting or travel operations.  

**Essential Safety Features for Mobile Cranes:**

1. **Load Moment Indicator (LMI) or Rated Capacity Limiter (RCL):**  
   Monitors the crane’s lifting load and alerts the operator if the load approaches or exceeds the safe lifting capacity, preventing overloading and potential tipping.

2. **Boom Angle and Length Indicators:**  
   These display real-time information about the boom’s angle and extension length to help the operator maintain safe working limits and avoid collisions with nearby structures or traffic routes.

3. **Anti-Two Block Device:**  
   Prevents the hook block from contacting the boom tip, which can cause catastrophic cable failure and load drops.

4. **Outriggers and Stability Alarms:**  
   Outriggers must have visual and audible signals to confirm they are fully deployed and locked. Some systems include sensors that prevent crane movement if outriggers are not properly extended, maintaining stability during lifts.

5. **Load Charts and Boom Configuration Displays:**  
   Allow the operator to select the correct lifting configuration based on boom length, radius, and counterweight setup—critical for preventing overloading and instability.

6. **Level Indicator:**  
   Ensures the crane is properly leveled before lifting, as uneven ground can significantly increase the risk of overturning, especially on roadways or sloped job sites.

7. **Wind Speed Indicator or Anemometer:**  
   Monitors wind conditions, which can severely affect crane stability and swing control, particularly on high-rise or open construction sites.

8. **Emergency Stop Systems:**  
   Located within easy reach of the operator and spotters, these allow immediate halting of crane operation during unsafe conditions or equipment failure.

9. **Automatic Safe Load Indicator (ASLI):**  
   Provides continuous feedback on load conditions—including boom angle, radius, and load weight—with alarms for unsafe conditions.

10. **Back-up Alarms, Cameras, and Lights:**  
   Since mobile cranes often move within active traffic zones or construction areas, these devices ensure visibility and warn nearby workers, vehicles, or pedestrians during operation or travel.

**Impact on Local Traffic and Site Safety:**  
When operating close to public roads, these safety features also mitigate risks to local traffic—by ensuring controlled slewing, stable positioning, and clear visibility around the crane. Proper use of load-limiting systems and alarms reduces the chance of dropped loads or overturning that could block or endanger adjacent roadways. Additionally, operators must coordinate with traffic management teams to establish exclusion zones and signal systems during crane movement.

In summary, mobile cranes must incorporate advanced mechanical, electronic, and procedural safety systems that safeguard both site operations and the surrounding public environment. Regular maintenance and operator training are equally critical to ensure those features function as intended.

   - Mobile cranes must be positioned on a level area to prevent tipping or excessive stresses on the structure.

   - Outriggers must be fully deployed to manufacturer specifications, often with steel plates or timber mats to spread the load.
