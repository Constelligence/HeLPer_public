---

**Qualifications:**
- Must hold a valid **Crane Operator License/Certificate** issued by the relevant regulatory authority (e.g., DOLE in the Philippines, OSHA/NCCCO in the USA, or other national accreditation bodies).  
- Must have completed **formal training** (theory and practical) from an accredited training provider.  
- Should have adequate **knowledge of crane operation principles**, load charts, signaling methods, and safety precautions.  
- Must undergo **medical examination** to ensure fitness for operating heavy machinery.  
- Should have **on-site experience** under supervision before being assigned to operate independently.  

---

### 2. **Rigger**
**Qualifications:**
- Must be a **trained and certified rigger**, competent in attaching and detaching loads, using rigging equipment, and inspecting slings, hooks, and lifting accessories.  
- Should have completed **rigging and slinging safety training**.  
- Must understand **load weight, balance, and communication protocols** with the crane operator.  

---

### 3. **Signalman / Banksman**
**Qualifications:**
- Must be **trained and certified** in standard hand and radio signals used in crane operations.  
- Should have **good visual and communication skills** to coordinate with the operator safely.  
- Knowledge of **site layout and load movement paths** is essential.  

---

### 4. **Lifting Supervisor (or Crane Supervisor)**
**Qualifications:**
- Must be **competent and experienced** in lifting operations, with understanding of crane capacity, ground conditions, and load planning.  
- Usually required to have completed **lifting operations management or supervisory training**.  
- Responsible for preparing or approving **Lifting Plans** and ensuring safety measures are implemented.  

---

### 5. **Erection and Dismantling Crew**
**Qualifications:**
- Must be **trained and authorized** for crane erection, climbing, and dismantling.  
- Should have adequate **knowledge of manufacturer procedures** and structural stability requirements.  
- Must use appropriate **fall protection equipment** and follow method statements and risk assessments.  

---

### 6. **Maintenance Personnel / Electricians**
**Qualifications:**
- Should be a **qualified mechanical or electrical technician/engineer** trained in crane maintenance and troubleshooting.  
- Must understand safe isolation, lock-out/tag-out, and inspection procedures.  

---

### 7. **Project Engineer / Safety Officer**
**Qualifications:**
- Must hold relevant **engineering or safety credentials** (Civil/Mechanical Engineer or Certified Safety Practitioner).

**Non-destructive testing (NDT)** refers to techniques used to evaluate the properties, integrity, or condition of a material, component, or structure **without causing damage**. These tests are performed by **qualified or certified personnel**—typically individuals certified according to standards such as **ASNT SNT-TC-1A**, **ISO 9712**, or **EN 473**.  

Here’s an overview of the **main types of NDT methods** commonly used by qualified personnel:

---

### **1. Visual Inspection (VT)**
- **Description:** The simplest form of NDT; involves visually examining surfaces for defects such as cracks, corrosion, misalignment, or surface irregularities.  
- **Tools:** Mirrors, magnifying glasses, borescopes, endoscopes, and advanced video systems.  
- **Applications:** Weld inspection, surface finishing checks, corrosion monitoring.  
- **Certification:** Visual inspectors must be trained in lighting, surface conditions, and defect characterization.

---

### **2. Radiographic Testing (RT)**
- **Description:** Uses X-rays or gamma rays to produce images of the internal structure of components.  
- **Purpose:** Reveals internal flaws like voids, cracks, inclusions, or weld defects.  
- **Equipment:** X-ray tubes, radioactive isotopes (Ir-192, Co-60), film or digital detectors.  
- **Safety:** Strict radiation safety and certification requirements.  

---

### **3. Ultrasonic Testing (UT)**
- **Description:** High-frequency sound waves are transmitted into the material; reflections are analyzed to detect internal flaws.  
- **Common Uses:** Thickness measurement, weld inspection, flaw detection, and material characterization.  
- **Advantages:** Can detect fine internal defects and measure depth accurately.  

---

### **4. Magnetic Particle Testing (MT or MPI)**
- **Description:** Used for detecting surface and near-surface discontinuities in ferromagnetic materials.  
- **Method:** Magnetic field is applied; ferromagnetic particles accumulate at discontinuities.  
- **Applications:** Weld inspection, castings, forgings, and structural components.  

---

### **5. Dye Penetrant Testing (PT or LPT)**
- **Description:** A dye solution penetrates surface-breaking defects; excess dye is removed, and a developer draws out remaining penetrant, revealing cracks.  
- **Suitable for:** Nonporous materials like metals, ceramics, and plastics.  
- **Uses:** Detection of surface flaws such as fatigue cracks or porosity.  

---

### **6. Eddy Current Testing (ET)**
- **Description:** Uses electromagnetic induction to detect surface and near-surface flaws in conductive materials.  
- **Applications:** Crack detection, coating thickness measurement, heat exchanger tube inspection.  
- **Advantages:** No need for coupling medium; works through thin coatings.  

---

### **7. Acoustic Emission Testing (AE)**
- **Description:** Monitors transient elastic waves from active defect sites under stress.  
- **Used for:** Detecting crack growth, corrosion activity, or leaks in pressure vessels.  

---

### **8. Leak Testing (LT)**
- **Description:** Detects and measures leakage in systems or components.  
- **Methods:** Bubble test, pressure-decay test, halogen leak detection, and mass spectrometer-based detection.  
- **Used for:** Pipelines, pressure vessels, tanks.  

---

### **9. Infrared Thermography (IRT)**
- **Description:** Uses thermal imaging to detect variations in temperature that may indicate defects, voids, or delaminations.  
- **Applications:** Electrical systems, composites, building inspections.

**Procedure:**

- Requires adequate counterweight and guy / tie systems when heights are substantial.

- The crane is initially installed at a low level inside the building structure, supported on a temporary frame or building floors.

- Support brackets are fixed at higher levels in the building to receive the crane.

- Protected from wind by the building structure.

3. The **climbing frame** (jack frame) temporarily lifts the entire slewing unit—machinery deck, counter jib, and working jib—slightly above the topmost mast section.
4. A new mast section is inserted into the gap between the top of the tower and the slewing unit.
5. The slewing unit is lowered onto the newly inserted section, and the process can be repeated as needed.

**Notes:**
- This can continue until the crane reaches its design maximum free-standing height.
- If required to go higher, the crane is often **tied to the building** for lateral stability.

---

### (B) *Internal Climbing Method*
Used when the crane is installed **within a building’s elevator shaft or internal core**.

**Process:**
1. The crane is supported on special beams or collars within the building structure.
2. As construction floors are completed, the crane uses its climbing frame to jack itself upward through an opening left in each floor.
3. A new mast section is added below the climbing frame (instead of above, as in the external method).
4. The completed floor below is closed once the crane advances upward.

**Advantages:**
- The crane can rise along with the building without the need for external bracing or ties.
- Wind loads on the crane are reduced, as the building provides shelter.

---

### (C) *Height Reduction (Dismantling)*
When construction is completed, height reduction (or “down-climbing”) is performed by reversing the process:
1. Remove building ties if any, in a planned sequence.
2. Use the climbing frame to lift and remove mast sections one by one.
3. Continue until only a small crane (sometimes a derrick or mobile crane on the roof) can remove the remaining sections and machine deck.

### Response 3

Mobile cranes are complex machines that handle heavy loads and require several safety features to ensure safe operation. The following are key safety features that should be provided on mobile cranes:

### 1. **Load Moment Indicator (LMI) or Rated Capacity Limiter (RCL)**
- Monitors the crane’s load and boom angle, and warns the operator if the crane is approaching or exceeding its rated capacity.
- Automatically restricts further unsafe movements when overload or tipping conditions are detected.

### 2. **Anti-Two Block Device**
- Prevents the hook block from being drawn up into the boom tip, which could damage the hoist rope or sheaves and cause rope failure.

### 3. **Boom Angle Indicator**
- Shows the boom’s angle relative to the horizontal, helping the operator determine radius and safe load limits.

### 4. **Boom Length Indicator**
- Displays the boom length, important for determining the operating radius and applying the correct load chart values.

### 5. **Load Charts and Placards**
- Clearly visible load charts should be provided, showing rated capacities for various boom lengths, angles, and configurations.

### 6. **Outrigger Interlocks and Indicators**
- Interlocking systems ensure crane operations are inhibited until outriggers are properly set.
- Indicators show deployment and leveling status of outriggers.

### 7. **Level Indicator**
- Ensures the crane is properly leveled before lifting; uneven leveling can severely reduce lifting capacity and stability.

### 8. **Audio-Visual Warning Devices**
- **Alarms and lights** alert personnel during movement, slewing, or load lifting.
- **Backup alarm** for mobile travel.

### 9. **Safe Load Indicator (SLI)**
- Warns the operator when the load approaches the maximum permissible value.

### 10. **Emergency Stop Switch**
- Allows the operator or ground personnel to stop crane movement instantly if an unsafe condition arises.

### 11. **Swing and Travel Locks**
- Prevents unwanted movement of the upper structure (rotation) or travel of the crane when parked or during maintenance.

### 12. **Wind Speed Indicator**
- Indicates prevailing wind speed and alerts when wind conditions exceed safe operating limits.

### 13. **Hydraulic Safety Valves and Check Valves**
- Prevent sudden load drops in case of hydraulic failure.

### 14. **Fail-safe Brakes and Hoist Mechanisms**
- Brakes must automatically engage in case of power loss to prevent uncontrolled load movement.

### 15. **Operator Cab Features**
- Good visibility and ergonomic controls.
- Safety glass, fire extinguisher, and seat belts.

### 16. **Lighting Systems**
- Sufficient illumination for nighttime or low-visibility operations.

### 17. **Communication Devices**
- Two-way radio or signal communication for coordination between the operator and signal person.

Safe mobile crane operation relies on a combination of mechanical, electronic, and procedural safety features: load monitoring (LMI/RCL), anti-two block devices, outrigger controls, warning alarms, safe load indicators, and structural safeguards all contribute to preventing accidents and ensuring operational stability.

In mobile crane operations, **"standing" or "supporting conditions"** refer to how the crane is set up on a site in order to safely lift loads. These conditions determine the stability of the crane and are critical for safe operations.  

Here are the **main standing/supporting conditions for mobile cranes** during setup or sitting:

---

### **1. On Outriggers (Fully Extended)**
- **Description**: Outriggers are extended to their maximum length and set on firm, level ground with appropriate cribbing or mats.
- **Purpose**: Provides maximum stability and lifting capacity because the crane’s base footprint is widened.
- **Surface requirements**: Firm, compacted soil or engineered surface that can bear the load without settling.
- **Usage**: Preferred for heavy lifts or when lifting charts specify maximum capacity.

---

### **2. On Outriggers (Partially Extended)**
- **Description**: Outriggers extended to a lesser than maximum span due to space constraints.
- **Purpose**: Used where full extension is not possible, but capacity is reduced according to the crane’s load chart.
- **Surface requirements**: Stable ground, proper cribbing still required.
- **Usage**: Urban jobsites, close quarters—must follow manufacturer’s specific load chart for this condition.

---

### **3. On Outriggers (Fully Retracted but Deployed)**
- **Description**: Outriggers are set down but not extended beyond the crane chassis width.
- **Purpose**: Minimal stability improvement; used mostly for light lifts or in transport position.
- **Capacity**: Significantly reduced compared to full extension.

---

### **4. On Wheels (Pick-and-Carry Operation)**
- **Description**: Crane remains on its tires without deploying outriggers; sometimes called 'on rubber'.
- **Purpose**: Allows the crane to travel with a load over short distances.
- **Capacity**: Much lower capacity; must use specific "on rubber" load chart.
- **Surface requirements**: Firm, level surfaces; good tire condition.
- **Risks**: Higher tipping potential—only certain cranes are rated for pick-and-carry.

---

### **5. Crawler Crane Supporting Condition**
- Although crawler cranes are not mobile cranes in the wheeled sense, they have their own standing conditions:
    - **On Tracks, Free-standing**: Stability comes from large track base; often used without outriggers.
    - **On Mats/Timber**: Spread the load on soft ground to avoid sinking.

- **Cribbing/Matting**: Outrigger pads or mats distribute load and prevent sinking; must be sized properly for the load.
- **Load Chart Compliance**: Always follow the manufacturer’s rated capacities for the specific standing condition.

### Response 3

When setting up (or “sitting”) a **mobile crane**, one of the most critical considerations is its **standing or supporting conditions**—that is, how the crane’s weight and loads are transferred safely to the ground. Improper ground conditions or support setup can lead to crane instability, tipping, or ground failure.  

Here’s a detailed breakdown of the **crane standing/support conditions** you should consider:

---

## 1. **Ground (Soil) Conditions**
Before positioning a crane, the **ground bearing capacity** must be assessed to ensure it can support the total load (crane self-weight + lifted load + dynamic effects).  
- **Typical checks:**
  - **Soil type:** clay, sand, gravel, compacted fill, asphalt, concrete, etc.
  - **Moisture/water table:** soft or waterlogged ground greatly reduces bearing capacity.
  - **Uniformity:** differences in soil type or compaction can create uneven support.
  - **Presence of underground services or voids:** can lead to localized failure.

- **Approximate minimum bearing capacities for mobile cranes:**  
  - Firm ground: ~200 kN/m²  
  - Compacted gravel: ~300–600 kN/m²  
  - Asphalt/concrete slab: ~500–1000 kN/m²  
  *(Always verify by engineering assessment or manufacturer guidance.)*

---

## 2. **Outrigger and Crawler Support**
Mobile cranes are stabilized using either **outriggers** (for truck or all-terrain cranes) or **crawler tracks** (for crawler cranes).  

### a) **Outrigger‑Based Cranes**
- All outriggers must be **fully extended and evenly deployed**, unless the manufacturer allows a reduced outrigger setup with specified lifting charts.
- Each outrigger pad transfers a **point load** to the ground, so **spreader mats or outrigger pads** are used to spread the load over a larger area.
- The **pad size and thickness** depend on:
  1. Outrigger load (from load charts).
  2. Ground bearing capacity.
  
  \[
  \text{Required pad area} = \frac{\text{Outrigger load}}{\text{Allowable ground bearing pressure}}
  \]

- Pads should be level, clean, and supported on compacted material.

### b) **Crawler Cranes**
- Crawler tracks distribute loads over a larger area, but the ground must still be firm and level.
- Soft spots under one track can cause tilting or excessive track sinkage.
- Load distribution mats or timber mats may be required under the tracks.

---

## 3. **Levelness**
- The crane must be set up **on level ground** (typically within ±1% to ±1.5° of level, depending on manufacturer specs).
- An out-of-level crane greatly reduces lifting capacity and can cause load swing or tipping.

---

## 4. **Support Materials**
Common support materials include:
- Timber crane mats (hardwood or laminated)
- Steel plates
- Engineered composite mats
- Concrete pads or reinforced slabs

These help spread loads and improve uniform contact.

---

## 5. **Environmental and Site Factors**
- **Rain or thawing conditions** can weaken ground after setup. Regularly re-assess conditions.
- **Adjacent excavations** or unstable edges** (e.g., near trenches or slopes) can undermine support.
  - Maintain safe distances from unsupported edges (often at least the depth of excavation away).

---

## 6. **Verification and Documentation**
- Ground bearing studies or lift plans should include:
  - Load data for each outrigger/crawler.
  - Ground bearing capacity verification.
  - Mat/pad design.
- A competent person should inspect and sign off before the lift.

---

### **In Summary:**
| Condition | Key Considerations |
|------------|--------------------|
| Ground | Firm, level, uniform, sufficient bearing capacity |
| Outriggers | Fully extended (unless rated otherwise), supported on adequate pads |
| Crawlers | Even, stable, non-slipping surface, mats if needed |
| Levelness | Within manufacturer tolerance |
| Environmental factors | Weather, excavation proximity, water, vibration effects |
| Verification | Engineering check, inspection, documentation |

---

**In essence:** The crane standing/supporting condition must ensure uniform, stable, and level support with ground bearing pressure not exceeding the soil’s capacity, achieved by appropriate outrigger or track support with mats or pads, verified and monitored throughout operations.
